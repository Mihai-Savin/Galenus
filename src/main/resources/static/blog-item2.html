SUMMER’S EAR: HOW TO PREVENT SEASONAL EAR CONDITIONS

Summertime brings lots of great outdoor activities like swimming, waterskiing and camping. While these are all wonderful past-times, each can carry a risk for an ear condition. Don’t worry: all are easily preventable and treatable if you follow these simple guidelines.


Swimmer’s Ear

Swimmer’s ear, also called external otitis, occurs when an ear becomes infected with bacteria that live in water. Often, it starts when water isn’t completely removed from the ear after swimming or if the water is polluted. Symptoms include itchy ears which can progress into soreness, pain when chewing and even swollen skin around the ear.

    Because this condition is caused by different bacteria than the kind that is typically treated with an oral antibiotic, a different antibiotic is required. Treatment:
  • Use antibiotic ear drops to inhibit bacterial growth. The drops usually relieve the pain within a day or two and clear up the condition within a week.
  • Don’t take oral antibiotics unless other conditions are present, such as diabetes or some immune diseases, because studies indicate those medications are less effective and have more side effects.
  • Don’t use ear candles which have not been shown to be effective and can have adverse side effects, including burning or perforating the ear drum.
  • Keep moisture out of the ear by using ear plugs or by drying the ears with a hairdryer, and avoiding water that may be polluted.
  • Don’t put fingers or other objects in the ear, including cotton swabs, which can damage the ear or push material deeper into the canal.
  • Use a couple drops of Swimmer’s Ear, an over-the-counter drop found at most drugstores, to dry out the ear after swimming. But, don’t use this if you or your child has tubes or a hole in the ear drum.


Insect in the Ear

This condition can occur anytime you are outdoors, especially in the buggy summertime. Usually, the person affected is frightened and should be reassured that it will come out.

Treatment:

  1. Do not let the person put a finger in the ear, since this may make the insect sting.
  2. Turn the person’s head so that the affected side is up, and wait to see if the insect flies or crawls out.
  3. If this doesn’t work, try pouring a small amount of mineral oil, olive oil, or baby oil into the ear. As you pour the oil, pull the ear lobe gently backward and upward for an adult, or backward and downward for a child. The insect should suffocate and may float out in the oil.
  4. Even if an insect appears to come out, get medical attention. Small insect parts can irritate the sensitive skin of the ear canal.
  5. If the person is stung, head for an emergency department or Ear, Nose and Throat specialist.


Waterskiing/tympanic membrane injury

During a waterskiing or wake boarding fall, the ear drum (tympanic membrane) can tear as a result of a “slap” to the ear. The ear canal is very thin and can’t handle impact. In 90% of cases, the tear will heal without surgical treatment. However, in rare cases, tympanoplasty may be required. This involves a small tissue graft take from the scalp to replace the tissue.

In the event of a waterskiing injury, doctors’ recommends that an ear, nose, and throat specialist examine the ear to check for hemotympanum (blood behind the ear) or a disconnection of the ear bones which can cause hearing loss. A physician may also recommend an antibiotic if water has gotten in past the tear.

Please note that this injury can also occur due to changes in water pressure during the descent of scuba divers.
